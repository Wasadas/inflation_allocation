## Writing style for comments and docs

### How we write

- Clarity and brevity  
  Write short sentences that focus on one idea. Aim for 10–20 words most of the time.
- Active voice and direct verbs  
  Say who does what. For example, write “The function loads prices” instead of “Prices are loaded”.
- Everyday vocabulary  
  Prefer simple words over abstract or corporate language.
- Straightforward punctuation  
  Use periods, commas, question marks, and colons for lists. Skip semicolons and long dashes.
- Varied sentence length  
  Mix short and medium sentences. Avoid long chains of clauses.
- Logical flow  
  Link thoughts with plain words such as “and”, “but”, “so”, and “then”.
- Concrete detail  
  Include numbers, dates, names, and ranges when they help the reader.
- Human cadence  
  Vary paragraph length. When you ask a question, answer it right away.

### What we avoid

- Punctuation  
  Do not use semicolons or em dashes in comments or docs.
- Buzzwords and stock phrases  
  Avoid phrases such as “paradigm shift”, “cutting‑edge”, “synergy”, “at the end of the day”, and similar slogans.
- Overused words and hedging  
  Avoid words like “however”, “moreover”, “ultimately”, “innovative”, “efficient”, “dynamic”, “ensure”, “leverage”, and “utilize”. Use plain verbs such as “use”, “measure”, or “change”.
- Long, tangled sentences  
  Avoid sentences with several clauses joined by many conjunctions. Split them into two or three clear steps.

### Simple examples

- Instead of: “We leveraged a dynamic, innovative framework to optimize performance.”  
  Use: “We used a simple model that runs faster.”

- Instead of: “Because the data were incomplete and the timeline was short, we postponed the launch, although we had secured funding.”  
  Use: “The data were incomplete. We had little time. We postponed the launch. Funding was ready.”

Comments should explain why a choice was made, not repeat what the code already shows.
